\chapter*{Abstract}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

This thesis explores the possible evolution of capitalism through the lens of decentralised 
artificial intelligence, with a focus on \textbf{Bittensor}~(\TAO)—a Decentralized peer-to-peer 
protocol that incentivises The Global Intelligence-Models/Code to compete and collaborate in 
an open, permissionless network, all striving to provide the best \textbf{Digital Commodity}.

Beginning with a historical overview of capitalism's major phases—from mercantilism 
and industrialism to financialised platform economies—the thesis examines how each 
era redefined value, labour, and ownership. It then investigates how Bittensor's 
architecture, consensus mechanism, and \textbf{Mining Implementation Mechanisms/Architecture} may constitute 
a structurally new phase in this evolution: one where intelligence itself becomes 
the commodity, and decentralised networks replace centralised institutions as the 
primary engines of value creation.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Bittensor, Decentralised AI, Capitalism, Evolution, Incentives, Yuma Consensus, Mining, Validating, Subnets, Tokenomics.

\chapter*{Background and Motivation}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Background and Motivation}


The motivation for this thesis did not originate in academic literature. It originated 
in observation—of systems, of incentives, and of the recurring patterns that emerge 
when human institutions fail to reward the people who drive them forward.\\

The first concern was concentration. As technology companies scaled, it became 
increasingly clear that the entities controlling the most valuable digital 
commodities—code, data, intelligence—had little structural incentive to share 
them, improve them, or relinquish control over them. Monopolisation, in this 
context, is not merely an economic outcome; it is the rational end-state of 
any system where ownership is centralised and competition is suppressed.\\

The counterexample came from \textbf{GitHub}. By making code openly accessible 
and attributable, GitHub demonstrated that transparency and shared ownership 
could unlock contributions at a scale no single company could replicate. 
Developers contributed not because they were told to, but because the system 
rewarded effort with visibility, reputation, and influence. Yet even this 
system operated within a broader economy defined by inflation, wage stagnation, 
and the persistent misalignment between contribution and compensation.\\

This led to \textbf{Bitcoin}—a protocol that replaced institutional trust with 
mathematical proof, and rewarded participants directly and transparently for 
the work they performed in maintaining the network. For the first time, an 
economic system existed in which the rules could not be arbitrarily changed 
by those in power providing true Digital Ownership.\\

Parallel observations emerged from platforms such as \textbf{Kaggle}, where 
data scientists compete openly to solve real problems, ranked by performance 
and rewarded accordingly. The model was simple but powerful: define a useful 
task, open it to everyone, reward the best result. The output was not merely 
a winning model—it was a global, self-organising meritocracy of intelligence.\\

The sharpest contrast, however, came from direct experience within conventional 
corporate structures. The defining feature of the modern firm is not efficiency—it 
is misalignment. Workers are rarely compensated in proportion to the value they 
generate. Incentive structures reward tenure over talent, compliance over 
creativity. Entire departments exist not to produce value, but to manage the 
friction created by the absence of genuine incentives. Human Resources, in 
many organisations, functions less as a mechanism for unlocking human potential 
and more as a bureaucratic bottleneck—a layer of administration interposed 
between ability and reward.\\

It was against this backdrop that \textbf{Bittensor}~(\TAO) came into focus: 
a protocol that takes the open competition of Kaggle, the trustless reward 
mechanism of Bitcoin, and the collaborative transparency of GitHub—and applies 
them to the production of Intelligence itself. On Bittensor, Models/Participants 
compete in open subnets, validators score their outputs, and emissions flow 
directly to those whose intelligence is deemed most useful by the network. 
There are no intermediaries, no HR departments, no opaque performance reviews. 
There is only contribution—and its verifiable reward.
All Participants are Incentivized to Be Honest and Produce the best Digital Commodity they can produce...\\

This thesis is an attempt to understand what that means for the future of 
capitalism itself.

\chapter{The History and Rise of Centralized Money}
\label{chap:history}

The history of money begins with simple systems of exchange, where goods and services 
were traded directly through barter. As societies expanded and economic activity became 
more complex, the need for a standardized medium of exchange led to the creation of 
early forms of money—objects such as grain, metal pieces, and later, minted coins. 
These forms of money held value because communities collectively agreed upon their 
worth, allowing trade to occur more efficiently and reliably.\\[0.4cm]

Over time, the authority to issue money shifted from individuals and merchants to 
centralized institutions. Ancient kingdoms and empires began minting coins stamped 
with the symbols of rulers, marking the emergence of money as a tool of state 
legitimacy and control. This process intensified with the development of banking 
systems, where early banks issued notes, safeguarded deposits, and facilitated 
large-scale transactions. As trust in these institutions grew, so did their influence 
over economic life.\\[0.4cm]

The rise of centralized money power accelerated with the creation of national banks 
and the establishment of government-backed currencies. Paper money, once a substitute 
for physical commodities like gold or silver, became the dominant form of currency as 
states asserted control over its issuance and regulation. This transition positioned 
central banks as key actors in economic management, with the ability to influence 
interest rates, credit availability, and overall financial stability.\\[0.4cm]

Through these developments, money evolved from a decentralized social agreement into 
a highly centralized mechanism governed by political and financial authorities. 
Centralized control over currency provided states with powerful tools for taxation, 
military funding, economic intervention, and social governance. By concentrating 
monetary authority, governments and financial institutions gained unprecedented 
influence over economic systems—marking a fundamental shift in how value, power, 
and economic activity were organized.\\[1.2cm]

\chapter{The Web3 Era}
\section{Decentralized Money}
\label{chap:decentralized}

The emergence of decentralized money began with the introduction of \textbf{Bitcoin} 
in 2009, proposed by the pseudonymous \textbf{Satoshi Nakamoto}. Bitcoin was a direct 
response to the vulnerabilities exposed by the \textbf{2008 Financial Crisis}—a moment 
that shattered public trust in banks, governments, and financial intermediaries. Its 
goal was simple: money that required no trusted third party, operated transparently, 
and could not be controlled or manipulated by any single entity.\\[0.4cm]

Bitcoin achieves this through a \textbf{distributed ledger}—the blockchain—where 
thousands of independent nodes collectively verify transactions using cryptographic 
proofs, without any central authority. The \textbf{Proof-of-Work} consensus mechanism 
drives this process: miners compete to solve mathematical puzzles, and the winner earns 
the right to add the next block and receive newly issued Bitcoin. Security and monetary 
policy are thus encoded directly into software.\\[0.4cm]

Bitcoin's supply is \textbf{capped at 21 million coins}—a fixed limit that stands in 
deliberate contrast to fiat currencies, which can be printed without bound. This 
enforced scarcity, combined with its decentralized architecture, established Bitcoin 
as \emph{digital gold}: a hedge against inflation and monetary manipulation, and the 
first asset in history to offer true self-custody at a global scale.\\[1.0cm]




Blockchain technology is widely misunderstood. The core idea—a peer-to-peer 
distributed network maintained by its own participants—did not originate with Bitcoin. 
Its earliest recognizable form was \textbf{torrenting}: a decentralized file-sharing 
protocol through which users simultaneously downloaded and contributed content, with 
no central server and no single point of control. People built and maintained this 
network not by mandate, but because the system \emph{aligned their incentives} to 
do so.\\[0.4cm]

Bitcoin took that same architecture and applied it to money. Just as a torrent node 
contributes bandwidth in exchange for access, a Bitcoin miner contributes computation 
in exchange for reward. The insight was not merely technical—it was economic. 
\textbf{Aligned incentives produce genuine innovation.} Bitcoin gave the world the 
first \emph{deflationary, decentralized, and truly self-custodied} hard money—not 
because institutions mandated it, but because the protocol made participation 
individually rational and collectively beneficial.

\section{Altcoins}
\label{chap:Altcoins}

Despite Bitcoin's revolutionary design, it was never optimally suited to replace 
fiat, Visa, or banking systems entirely. As stated in Nakamoto's original whitepaper, 
Bitcoin was conceived as a \textbf{peer-to-peer electronic cash system}—yet its 
prioritisation of security and decentralization came at the cost of transaction 
throughput. Its slow \textbf{TPS (Transactions Per Second)} made it poorly equipped, 
on its own, to serve the demands of global everyday commerce.\\[0.4cm]

The \textbf{Web3 era} represents the expansion of blockchain technology beyond digital 
money into programmable, decentralized applications. Early projects such as Litecoin, 
Ripple (XRP), and Dogecoin explored variations in speed, supply mechanics, and use 
cases. Later networks—Cardano, Polkadot, Solana—introduced scalable architectures 
and new economic models, shifting the focus from simple value transfer to broader 
decentralized ecosystems.\\[0.4cm]

Among all these developments, \textbf{Ethereum} played the most pivotal role. 
Launched in 2015, Ethereum introduced \textbf{smart contracts}—self-executing programs 
stored on the blockchain—transforming blockchains from static ledgers into fully 
expressive global computation platforms. This enabled decentralized applications 
(dApps), DeFi, NFTs, and thousands of programmable economic systems. Ethereum's later 
transition from \textbf{Proof-of-Work to Proof-of-Stake} significantly reduced energy 
consumption, increased TPS, and laid the foundation for further scaling upgrades such 
as sharding. It remains the dominant smart-contract platform and central building block 
of Web3.


\label{sec:networks}

As the ecosystem matured, distinct networks emerged—each making deliberate trade-offs 
between speed, security, decentralization, and privacy.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Ethereum} (PoS, Smart-Contract Pioneer) prioritizes decentralization, security, 
and developer adoption. Layer-2 rollups now handle most transaction throughput, 
positioning Ethereum as the settlement and coordination layer of Web3 rather than a 
high-TPS execution chain.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Solana} (PoS + Proof of History) is designed for maximum throughput and low 
latency. Its unique Proof of History mechanism enables very high TPS and low fees, 
making it well-suited for real-time applications—DeFi, NFTs, and on-chain gaming—at 
the cost of higher hardware requirements.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Kaspa} (PoW, blockDAG Architecture) reimagines Proof-of-Work by replacing 
the traditional linear chain with a \textbf{blockDAG (GHOSTDAG)} structure, allowing 
multiple blocks to be produced and confirmed in parallel. This enables approximately 
one-second block times while preserving PoW security and decentralization—widely 
considered a serious attempt at solving the \emph{Blockchain Trilemma} of speed, 
scalability, and decentralization.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Sui} (PoS, Object-Based Execution) enables parallel transaction execution 
through an object-based data model, achieving high throughput and low confirmation 
times—optimized for games and consumer-facing applications.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Monero} (PoW, Privacy-First) uses ring signatures, stealth addresses, and 
confidential transactions to ensure that senders, receivers, and amounts remain 
private by default. It is the only major cryptocurrency used natively for private 
peer-to-peer transactions, including on dark web markets—a distinction that 
underscores both its utility and its controversy.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Zcash} (PoW, Selective Privacy) introduced \textbf{zk-SNARKs}—zero-knowledge 
proofs—as the first blockchain to implement them correctly, allowing users to 
optionally shield transactions, hiding sender, receiver, and amount 
mathematically, while retaining the option of full transparency.

version of the ledger across thousands of independent nodes. It determines who gets 
to add new blocks, which transactions are valid, and how the network resolves 
conflicts—all without relying on a central authority.\\[0.4cm]

\begin{center}

\section{Decentralized Intelligence (Web4)}
\label{sec:web4}

The natural and most logical path in the evolution of digital technologies is the 
convergence of decentralized systems with the most valuable and promising advancement 
of our lifetime: Artificial Intelligence. As blockchain matured—from decentralized 
money to programmable networks—integrating AI into these systems became an inevitable 
next step. The idea of combining distributed incentives, open participation, and global 
coordination with intelligent computation attracted significant interest across the 
industry.\\[0.4cm]

Many Blockchains attempted to merge with AI, exploring visions of decentralized 
model training, data marketplaces, and AI governance. However, most struggled with 
scalability, economic viability, or accurately describign what intelligence or model should be valued. Among these efforts, 
\textbf{Bittensor}~(\TAO) emerged as the most coherent and promising implementation—
successfully aligning blockchain incentives with the production of high-quality machine 
intelligence. It built an \textbf{Incentive Layer}: a permissionless system in which anyone 
with the right knowledge and expertise can participate, contribute, and be rewarded—
entirely bypassing centralised means of gatekeeping such as location-based hiring, 
slow institutional processes, and opaque performance evaluation.\\[0.4cm]

At its core, Bittensor represents what can be described as \textbf{Web4}: the era of 
decentralized intelligence. Participants contribute machine learning models—or whatever 
computational solution the network demands—validators assess their outputs, and the 
network issues \TAO\ tokens based on performance. Intelligence itself becomes an 
\emph{economic resource and commodity}.\\[0.4cm]

This architecture eliminates the need for a centralised AI provider. Instead, the 
network becomes a simultaneously \textbf{competitive and collaborative} marketplace 
of models, each improving through continuous incentive-driven evolution. Bittensor's 
design mirrors \emph{natural selection}: high-performing intelligence is rewarded, 
weaker models fall behind, and the system grows more capable over time.\\[0.4cm]

By enabling anyone in the world to contribute or access intelligence, Bittensor 
establishes intelligence as a \textbf{decentralized public good}. It extends the logic 
of Bitcoin and Ethereum into a new domain—where not only value or computation is 
decentralized, but the \emph{creation of intelligence itself}. Bittensor was never 
solely intended for machine learning goods. It was designed for any problem that can 
be rigorously expressed and assessed:

\begin{center}
\textit{``If you can express your problem and the assessment well enough,}\\
\textit{the network will solve the problem for you.''}
\end{center}







\section{The Network}
\label{sec:network}

\subsection{Architecture and Participants}

Bittensor's network is not a single monolithic blockchain. It is a \textbf{layered 
ecosystem} of specialised sub-networks, each dedicated to a specific problem or 
domain of intelligence. At the top level sits the \textbf{root network}, which 
governs the allocation of \TAO\ emissions across the entire ecosystem. Beneath it 
operate hundreds of independent \textbf{Subnets}—each one a self-contained 
competitive market for a specific type of intelligence or digital commodity. Bittensor is Inflationary by design especially in its early days so it can fund its own Subnets and pay the participants through Emissions.\\[0.4cm]

The network is composed of four core participants:

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{Subnet Owners}] are the architects of their own Sub-System(Subnet). They define the 
  problem, design the incentive mechanism, and deploy the subnet. In economic terms, 
  they function as \emph{entrepreneurs-Company}—identifying a valuable problem, structuring 
  the rules of competition, and attracting participants to solve it. A subnet owner's 
  long-term reward is tied directly to the quality and utility of their subnet.

  \item[\textbf{Miners}] are the producers of intelligence. They run computational 
  nodes—models, algorithms, or any software capable of responding to the subnet's 
  task—and compete to provide the best possible output(Based on the Subnet's Incentive Mechanism-Optimizer). Miners are rewarded in \TAO\ 
  proportional to the quality of their contributions as assessed by validators. 
  Critically, there is no fixed salary, no tenure, and no bureaucratic review—only 
  verifiable performance.

  \item[\textbf{Validators}] are the quality arbiters of the network. They query 
  miners, evaluate their outputs, and assign score Matrixes that determine the distribution 
  of emissions. Validators stake \TAO\ as collateral, aligning their economic 
  incentives with honest and accurate assessment. A validator that scores dishonestly 
  or carelessly risks losing both reputation and stake.

  \item[\textbf{Stakers (Delegators)}] are the capital layer of the network. Rather 
  than running nodes or evaluating outputs directly, stakers delegate their \TAO\ to 
  validators they trust, earning a proportional share of the validator's emissions in 
  return. Staking serves two functions simultaneously: it \emph{secures the network} 
  by increasing the economic cost of dishonest validation, and it \emph{democratises 
  participation}—allowing anyone who holds \TAO\ to contribute to and benefit from 
  the network's growth without requiring technical expertise. In economic terms, 
  stakers function as \emph{passive capital allocators}, voting with their stake on 
  which validators—and by extension, which subnets—deserve to grow.

  
\end{description}


\subsection{Subnets as Companies, Miners as Workers}

The most intuitive analogy for understanding Bittensor's structure is that of a 
\textbf{decentralized labour market}. Each subnet functions like an independent 
company: it has a defined purpose, a set of performance standards, and a budget—
denominated in \TAO\ emissions—that it distributes to those who contribute most 
effectively.\\[0.4cm]

Miners operate within this structure as workers—but workers in a fundamentally 
different sense from the conventional employment model. There is no hiring process as we know it, 
no geographic restriction, and no fixed compensation. A miner anywhere in the world 
can join any subnet, deploy a node, and begin earning immediately if their 
contribution meets the network's standard and thus producing Intelligence. The incentive is direct, transparent, 
and proportional to output.\\[0.4cm]

This stands in sharp contrast to the centralised firm, where compensation is 
decoupled from marginal contribution, and where institutional friction—interviews, 
hierarchies, HR departments—interposes itself between talent and reward. On 
Bittensor, \textbf{the market itself is the employer, and performance is the 
only credential that matters}.

\subsection{How Bittensor Differs from Traditional Blockchain Architectures}

Bittensor's relationship with its participants is fundamentally unlike that of 
Bitcoin, Ethereum, or any conventional blockchain network. In traditional systems, 
miners and validators perform a \textbf{fixed, well-defined task}: hash a block, 
validate a transaction, attest to a slot. The work is repetitive, standardised, 
and does not require constant adaptation over time. A Bitcoin miner running the same 
hardware and software in 2015 and in 2025 is performing essentially the same 
operation.\\[0.4cm]

Bittensor inverts this entirely. Because each subnet defines its own task—and 
because those tasks are competitive and continuously evolving—\textbf{miners must 
constantly improve their models, update their code, and adapt their nodes} to remain 
competitive. A miner who deploys a model and leaves it unchanged will inevitably be 
outperformed by those who iterate. The network does not reward existence—it rewards 
\emph{improvement}.\\[0.4cm]

This creates a self-reinforcing dynamic of continuous innovation. Unlike proof-of-work 
mining, where the barrier to entry is primarily capital (hardware), Bittensor's 
barrier to sustained performance is \textbf{expertise and adaptability}. The network 
naturally selects for participants who understand the problem deeply enough to keep 
improving their solution—transforming the act of participation into an ongoing 
research and development process.\\[0.4cm]

In this sense, Bittensor is less analogous to a blockchain and more analogous to a 
\textbf{living market for intelligence}—one that grows more capable precisely because 
its incentive structure demands it.







\section{Tokenomics}
\label{sec:tokenomics}

\subsection{TAO: The Native Token}

Bittensor's economic architecture is anchored by \textbf{\TAO}, the network's native 
token. By design, Bittensor adopts Bitcoin's fair-launch principles: no venture capital 
allocation, no pre-mine, and a \textbf{hard supply cap of 21 million \TAO}. The network is owned by the people 
providing value to it, not by a central board of institutional 
investors. Every \TAO\ in existence was earned through contribution to the network. Subnets also have their own Token called \textbf{Alpha}. it has the exact same tokenomics as \TAO\.

\subsection{Halvings}



\subsubsection*{What is a Halving?}

A halving is a pre-programmed event encoded directly into the protocol in which the 
rate of new token emissions is cut in half. It is not a decision made by any 
authority—it is a mathematical certainty, triggered automatically when a specific 
supply threshold is reached. The purpose is twofold: to enforce \textbf{scarcity} 
as adoption grows, and to transition the network from an incentive-heavy bootstrapping 
phase toward a mature, utility-driven economy. Each halving makes every previously 
earned token more scarce—and therefore, assuming sustained or growing demand, 
more valuable.

\subsubsection*{Two Halving Events in Bittensor}

Uniquely, Bittensor operates with \textbf{two distinct and independent halving 
mechanisms}—one at the network level and one at the subnet level:

\begin{description}
  \item[\textbf{TAO Halving (Network-Level)}] governs the global emission rate of 
  \TAO\ across the entire network. The first TAO halving occurred in 
  \textbf{December 2025}, reducing daily emissions from \textbf{7,200 \TAO} to 
  \textbf{3,600 \TAO}. This marked the network's transition from its expansion 
  phase to its scarcity phase. Subsequent halvings will continue compressing 
  global emissions over time, mirroring Bitcoin's deflationary monetary trajectory. Therefore TAO Halving happens everytime supply Doubles.

  \item[\textbf{Alpha Token Halving (Subnet-Level)}] governs the emission rate of 
  each individual subnet's Alpha token ($\alpha$). Critically, each subnet's halving 
  is \textbf{independent}—triggered not by a global clock, but by that subnet's own 
  internal supply milestone. Each time a subnet's circulating Alpha supply 
  \textbf{doubles}, its emission rate is halved. This means that older, more 
  established subnets with larger circulating supplies will halve sooner, while 
  newer subnets remain in their expansion phase longer—creating a natural 
  \emph{lifecycle} for each subnet's economy.
\end{description}

This dual-halving architecture means that scarcity operates at two levels 
simultaneously: globally across the entire Bittensor network, and locally within 
each individual subnet market. The result is a richly layered deflationary system 
in which both \TAO\ and each subnet's Alpha token are subject to their own 
independent supply constraints—aligning long-term incentives for every participant 
at every level of the network.




\subsection{Emissions}

Emissions are the mechanism by which new \TAO\ is introduced into circulation. 
Rather than being minted arbitrarily or distributed by a central authority, 
emissions are \textbf{algorithmically released}(3600 \TAO\ each day currently)  by the protocol at a fixed rate 
and allocated directly to network participants based on their verified contribution.\\[0.4cm]

Every block produced on the Bittensor blockchain generates a fixed quantity of 
new \TAO, which flows first to the \textbf{root network}—the top-level  
layer—and is then allocated downward to individual subnets proportional to the 
amount of \TAO\ staked into each subnet's pool. A subnet that attracts more 
staked capital receives a larger share of the global emissions. This creates a 
direct market signal: \textbf{capital flows toward subnets producing genuine 
utility} (just like in our own capitalistic economy), and emissions follow capital.\\[0.4cm]

Within each subnet, received emissions are then distributed among its four 
productive participants—subnet owners, miners, validators and stakers. Emissions therefore serve not only as a 
monetary policy tool, but as the \textbf{primary incentive signal} of the 
entire network: every participant, at every level, is working to maximise 
their share of the next block's emission and get rewarded more than the others.

\subsection{Subnet Alpha Tokens (\texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha})}

With the introduction of \textbf{Dynamic TAO}, each subnet was assigned its own 
native token—the \textbf{Alpha token ($\alpha$)}—also hard-capped at 21 million 
units. This created a two-layer economic architecture: \TAO\ as the global reserve 
currency of the network, and Alpha tokens as subnet-level instruments that reflect 
the specific utility and demand of each subnet's market.\\[0.4cm]

Each subnet operates an internal \textbf{Automated Market Maker (AMM)}—analogous 
to a Uniswap liquidity pool—through which participants exchange \TAO\ for a subnet's 
Alpha tokens (unlike traditional markets that rely on an order book). The price of 
a subnet's Alpha token is determined entirely by market forces, governed by the 
\textbf{constant product formula}:

\[
  k = \tau_{\text{pool}} \times \alpha_{\text{pool}}
\]



Where $k$ is a constant, $\TAO_{\text{pool}}$ is the amount of \TAO\ in the pool, 
and $\alpha_{\text{pool}}$ is the amount of Alpha tokens in the pool. The spot price 
of one Alpha token in terms of \TAO\ is therefore:

\[
  P_{\alpha} = \frac{\tau_{\text{pool}}}{\alpha_{\text{pool}}}
\]

When a participant stakes \TAO\ into the pool, $\TAO_{\text{pool}}$ increases, 
$\alpha_{\text{pool}}$ decreases, and the price of Alpha rises. When they 
unstake, the reverse occurs. No counterparty is needed—the math is the 
market maker.\\[0.4cm]

This mechanism transforms subnet valuation into a \emph{continuous market 
referendum} on which subnets are producing genuine utility: capital flows 
in, price rises, emissions follow.

The implications are significant. Capital flows naturally toward high-performing 
subnets, increasing their Alpha token price and attracting more miners and validators. 
Underperforming subnets face capital outflows, reduced emissions, and competitive 
pressure to improve or be replaced. \textbf{The market itself becomes the Price  regulator}.

\subsection{Bittensor as Pure Capitalism}

The architecture described above represents capitalism in its most structurally 
coherent form. There are no barriers to entry beyond the ability to contribute. 
There are no gatekeepers, no preferential allocations, and no intermediaries 
extracting rent between producer and reward. The supply is fixed, the rules are 
transparent, and the incentives are mathematically enforced.\\[0.4cm]











\section{Yuma Consensus}

Yuma Consensus is the mechanism Bittensor uses to determine \textbf{who gets paid 
and how much}. It is not a block-production consensus like Bitcoin's Proof-of-Work 
or Ethereum's Proof-of-Stake. Its sole purpose is to produce a \textbf{fair, 
manipulation-resistant distribution of emissions} across miners—based on the 
collective judgement of validators.\\[0.4cm]

\subsubsection*{How It Works}


Yuma Consensus runs in five sequential steps each tempo:

\textbf{Step 1 — Stake Normalisation}\\
Each validator's stake is normalised so all stakes sum to 1:
\[
  s_v = \frac{\tau_v}{\sum_{v} \tau_v}
\]

\textbf{Step 2 — Rank}\\
Each miner receives a rank: the stake-weighted sum of all validator scores:
\[
  R_m = \sum_{v} s_v \cdot W_{v,m}
\]

\textbf{Step 3 — Trust}\\
A miner's trust reflects how consistently validators agree on their quality:
\[
  T_m = \sum_{v} s_v \cdot \mathbb{1}\left[W_{v,m} > 0\right]
\]
A miner scored by more high-stake validators has higher trust.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Step 4 — Incentive}\\
A miner's final emission share combines rank and trust:
\[
  I_m = R_m \cdot T_m
\]

\textbf{Step 5 — Validator Dividends}\\
Each validator's reward is proportional to their bond-weighted share of miner incentives:
\[
  D_v = \sum_{m} B_{v,m} \cdot I_m
\]

\noindent Each step builds on the previous — stake determines rank, rank combined 
with trust determines incentive, and bonds determine how much of that incentive 
each validator captures.




\subsubsection*{Why It Is Manipulation-Resistant}

The critical insight of Yuma Consensus is that it \textbf{penalises validators 
who deviate from the consensus}. A validator that assigns weights very differently 
from the rest of the validator set—whether out of dishonesty, collusion, or 
negligence—receives reduced emissions themselves. This creates a powerful 
self-correcting dynamic:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Honest validators who assess miners accurately are rewarded.
  \item Validators who attempt to artificially boost specific miners at the 
        expense of others are penalised.
  \item Collusion requires controlling a majority of stake—making attacks 
        economically expensive.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{In Simple Terms}

Yuma Consensus can be understood as a \textbf{stake-weighted peer review system}. 
Validators are the reviewers, miners are the researchers, and \TAO\ emissions are 
the funding. The network rewards work that the majority of trusted reviewers 
agree is valuable—and punishes anyone who tries to game the review process. 
The result is a system where \textbf{honesty is the most profitable strategy}.








\subsection{Dynamic TAO}

Prior to Dynamic TAO, the allocation of emissions across subnets was determined 
by a small set of root network validators—a centralised bottleneck that created 
political pressure and misaligned incentives at the top level of the network.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Dynamic TAO removed this bottleneck entirely.} Subnet emission allocation 
is now determined by the market itself—specifically, by how much \TAO\ is staked 
into each subnet's Alpha token pool. More staked \TAO\ means a higher subnet 
valuation, which means a larger share of global emissions.\\[0.4cm]

In simple terms: \textbf{the market votes with capital}. Subnets that produce 
genuine utility attract stakers, which increases their emissions, which attracts 
better miners and validators—a self-reinforcing cycle of quality improvement. 
Subnets that fail to deliver lose capital, lose emissions, and are forced to 
improve or die.

\section{Issues and Incentive-Based Solutions}
\label{sec:issues}

No decentralised network is immune to adversarial behaviour. Bittensor's design 
anticipates the rational self-interest of every participant and encodes solutions 
directly into its economic architecture. The following are the most significant 
challenges the network faces—and how the protocol handles each.

\subsection{Weight Copying}

\subsubsection*{The Problem}

Weight copying is the most pervasive attack on Bittensor's validator layer. 
Rather than performing the computationally expensive work of querying miners, 
evaluating their outputs, and forming independent judgements, a lazy or malicious 
validator simply \textbf{copies the weights submitted by another validator}. 
This is economically rational in the short term: the validator receives emissions 
without doing any real work.\\[0.4cm]

If left unchecked, weight copying would cause the entire validator layer to 
converge on a single set of weights—undermining the diversity of evaluation that 
makes Yuma Consensus meaningful, and effectively centralising the network's 
judgement into whoever is being copied.

\subsubsection*{The Solution: Bonds}

Bittensor addresses weight copying through a \textbf{bonds mechanism}. Each 
validator accumulates a bond in every miner they score. A bond represents a 
validator's historical commitment to a miner—built up over time through 
consistent, independent evaluation.\\[0.4cm]

The bond of validator $v$ in miner $m$ at time $t$ is updated as:

\[
  B_{v,m}^{(t)} = (1 - \alpha) \cdot B_{v,m}^{(t-1)} + \alpha \cdot W_{v,m}^{(t)}
\]

Where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $B_{v,m}^{(t)}$ is the bond of validator $v$ in miner $m$ at time $t$
  \item $W_{v,m}^{(t)}$ is the weight validator $v$ assigned to miner $m$ at time $t$
  \item $\alpha$ is the bond moving average decay rate
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.4cm}
The validator's share of emissions is then proportional not just to their stake, 
but to their \textbf{bond-weighted consensus score}:

\[
  \text{Validator Dividend}_{v} \propto \sum_{m} B_{v,m} \cdot C_{m}
\]

Where $C_{m}$ is the consensus score of miner $m$.\\[0.4cm]

The critical implication is this: \textbf{a validator who copies weights builds 
no independent bonds}. They accumulate the same bonds as whoever they are copying—
but always with a lag. When a genuinely useful new miner enters the network, the 
validator who discovers them first and scores them highly builds a bond immediately. 
The copier, by definition, is always late. Early bond accumulation in high-performing 
miners translates directly into higher long-term dividends—making \textbf{independent 
discovery the most profitable strategy}.

\subsection{Validator Collusion}

\subsubsection*{The Problem}

A group of validators could theoretically collude to artificially inflate the 
scores of specific miners—miners they control—concentrating emissions among 
themselves and excluding legitimate competitors.

\subsubsection*{The Solution: Stake-Weighted Consensus}

Yuma Consensus computes each miner's final score as a \textbf{stake-weighted 
median} of all validator weight submissions:

\[
  C_{m} = \text{WeightedMedian}_{v}\left(W_{v,m},\ \tau_{v}\right)
\]

Where $\tau_{v}$ is the total stake of validator $v$. The median—rather than 
the mean—is used precisely because it is \textbf{resistant to outliers}. A 
colluding minority cannot pull the consensus score significantly unless they 
control a majority of total stake. Acquiring that majority stake is 
prohibitively expensive, making large-scale collusion economically irrational.

\subsection{Miner Feedback Gaming}

\subsubsection*{The Problem}

A miner could attempt to identify which validator is querying them and return 
artificially high-quality responses only to that validator—gaming their score 
without consistently performing well for actual users of the subnet.

\subsubsection*{The Solution: Validator Anonymity and Randomisation}

Validators are not required to identify themselves when querying miners. 
Queries are randomised in timing and structure, making it statistically 
difficult for a miner to reliably distinguish a validator query from a 
genuine user request. A miner that attempts selective performance must 
essentially perform well \emph{all the time}—which is indistinguishable 
from simply being a good miner.

\subsection{Subnet Owner Misalignment}

\subsubsection*{The Problem}

A subnet owner could design a poor or deliberately skewed incentive mechanism 
that rewards low-quality miners—friends, sockpuppets, or their own nodes—
capturing the 18\% owner emission share without producing genuine utility.

\subsubsection*{The Solution: Market Discipline via Dynamic TAO}

Under Dynamic TAO, a subnet's emission share is determined by how much \TAO\ 
is staked into its pool. A subnet producing no genuine utility attracts no 
stakers—its Alpha token price stagnates or falls, its emission share shrinks, 
and it becomes economically unviable. The subnet owner's 18\% of a shrinking 
emission stream is worth less than an honest owner's 18\% of a growing one.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{The market punishes bad subnet design automatically}—no governance 
intervention required.

\section{What is a Subnet?}

A Subnet is a \textbf{specialised, self-contained competitive market} built on 
top of the Bittensor protocol, dedicated to a specific computational task or 
digital commodity. If Bittensor is the global brain, a subnet is a specific 
functional lobe—purpose-built, independently governed, and ruthlessly competitive.\\[0.4cm]

Each subnet defines its own problem, its own metric of success, and its own 
incentive mechanism. Miners compete to solve the problem. Validators judge the 
quality of solutions. Emissions reward the winners. There is no fixed salary, 
no guaranteed participation, and no protection from being outperformed.\\[0.4cm]

This architecture deliberately mirrors \textbf{Darwinian natural selection}. 
Every tempo, every miner's output is evaluated and scored. Those who perform 
best capture the largest share of emissions. Those who fall behind receive less 
and less until participation becomes economically unviable. There is no tenure, 
no loyalty reward, and no institutional protection. The subnet does not care 
who you are—only what you produce. Models that do not improve are replaced by 
models that do. Subnets that do not attract capital and talent stagnate and 
die.\\[0.4cm]

This is not a metaphor. It is the \textbf{literal economic structure} of the 
network: continuous selection pressure, applied every twelve seconds, across 
every subnet simultaneously. The result is a system that grows more capable 
over time not because anyone planned it to, but because the incentive structure 
makes improvement the only rational long-term strategy.

\section{How Subnets Should Work}

A well-functioning subnet follows a clear operational logic. The subnet owner 
defines a \textbf{precise, measurable task}—one where good and bad outputs can 
be objectively distinguished. Validators query miners, score their outputs 
according to the owner's incentive mechanism, and submit weights to the 
blockchain. Yuma Consensus aggregates those weights into consensus scores, and 
emissions flow to miners proportionally.\\[0.4cm]

The quality of a subnet is almost entirely determined by the quality of its 
\textbf{incentive mechanism}. A well-designed mechanism creates a virtuous 
cycle: clear evaluation criteria attract serious miners, serious miners produce 
high-quality outputs, high-quality outputs attract stakers, stakers increase 
the subnet's emission share, and higher emissions attract even better miners. 
Conversely, a poorly designed mechanism—one with ambiguous scoring, gameable 
metrics, or misaligned rewards—produces a race to the bottom.\\[0.4cm]

The subnet owner's 18\% emission share is therefore not passive income. It is 
a \textbf{continuous incentive to maintain and improve the mechanism}. An owner 
who neglects their subnet watches its Alpha token price fall, its emission share 
shrink, and its best miners migrate to more rewarding subnets.

\section{Subnet Lifecycle}

\textbf{Registration.} Any participant can register a new subnet by paying a 
registration fee denominated in \TAO. This fee is burned, reducing circulating 
supply. As demand for subnet slots grows, the registration cost increases 
dynamically—ensuring that only subnets with genuine economic conviction enter 
the network.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Bootstrapping.} A newly registered subnet begins with zero miners, 
zero validators, and zero staked capital. The subnet owner must attract 
participants by designing a compelling incentive mechanism and demonstrating 
the utility of the task. Early emissions are low, but so is competition—
creating an opportunity window for early miners to accumulate bonds and 
establish dominance.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Growth.} As the subnet proves its utility, stakers allocate \TAO\ 
to its Alpha pool. Its emission share grows. More miners and validators 
register. Competition intensifies. The quality of outputs improves. The 
Alpha token price rises as a market signal of the subnet's value to the 
broader network.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Maturity.} A mature subnet operates as a stable, competitive market 
with established validators, a diverse miner set, and consistent output 
quality. Its Alpha token has undergone at least one halving, signalling that 
a significant portion of its supply has been distributed to productive 
participants.\\[0.4cm]

\textbf{Death.} A subnet that fails to produce genuine utility loses stakers, 
loses emission share, and loses miners to better opportunities. Without 
capital inflow, its Alpha token price collapses. Eventually, participation 
becomes economically irrational and the subnet is effectively abandoned. 
The market has rendered its verdict—no governance vote required.

\chapter{The Evolution of Capitalism}
\label{chap:evolution}

Capitalism has never been a single, fixed system. It is a \textbf{living 
architecture}—one that adapts, mutates, and reorganises itself around whatever 
technology most efficiently produces and concentrates value. Each phase of its 
evolution has been defined by a new answer to the same fundamental question: 
\textit{who controls the means of production, and how are the gains distributed?}\\[0.4cm]

From \textbf{mercantile capitalism}, where value was extracted through trade 
monopolies and colonial control, to \textbf{industrial capitalism}, where it 
was extracted through ownership of machinery and the labour of wages—to 
\textbf{financial capitalism}, where it was extracted through the control of 
credit, derivatives, and platform monopolies. In every phase, the pattern is 
the same: a new technology emerges, a new class captures it, and the gains 
concentrate upward.\\[0.4cm]

The digital economy accelerated this pattern to its logical extreme. A handful 
of technology companies—controlling data, distribution, and now artificial 
intelligence—have achieved a degree of economic concentration that mercantile 
monopolists could only dream of. The means of production in the twenty-first 
century is \textbf{intelligence}, and it is almost entirely privately owned.\\[0.4cm]

Bittensor does not merely propose a new technology. It proposes a new 
\textbf{ownership structure for the means of intelligence production}—one 
in which the gains flow directly to those who produce the value, governed 
by mathematics rather than management. In every prior phase of capitalism, 
the worker—the person actually producing value—received the smallest share 
of the output they generated. Bittensor's architecture inverts this: emissions 
flow directly to miners, validators, and stakers in proportion to their verified 
contribution. There is no board of directors extracting surplus, no opaque 
performance review determining compensation. The protocol \emph{is} the 
employment contract—and it is mathematically enforced.\\[0.4cm]

What makes this transition historically significant is the nature of the 
commodity being produced. Every prior capitalist phase produced \emph{physical} 
or \emph{financial} goods—objects, energy, credit. Bittensor produces 
\textbf{intelligence}: the capacity to solve problems, generate knowledge, 
and automate cognition at scale. If intelligence becomes the dominant factor 
of production in the coming decades—and there is substantial evidence that 
it will—then the question of \emph{who owns the means of intelligence 
production} becomes the defining political-economic question of our time. 
Bittensor's answer is unambiguous: \textbf{no one owns it, and everyone 
can contribute to it}.\\[0.4cm]

The closest historical analogy is \textbf{open source software}. Just as 
Linux, Python, and GitHub allowed any developer anywhere in the world to 
take existing code, build upon it, and contribute improvements back to the 
collective—Bittensor extends this principle to intelligence itself. A miner 
does not need to build from scratch. They can take an existing open model, 
stand on the shoulders of every researcher who came before them, extend it 
with their own ideas, and deploy it into the network. The accumulated 
knowledge of humanity becomes the starting point, not the finish line.\\[0.4cm]

But open source alone has a ceiling. GitHub democratised contribution—yet 
without economic incentives, it could only go so far. The reality of open 
source is that the vast majority of its value is captured by corporations 
that build proprietary products on top of free community labour. The 
contributors are rarely the beneficiaries. \textbf{Bittensor supercharges 
the open source model by doing what GitHub never could: paying people for 
their work.} Miners are economically rewarded for deploying better models. 
Validators are rewarded for identifying them. The entire network is aligned 
toward a single goal—producing the best possible intelligence—because that 
goal is also individually profitable for every participant. This is what 
cryptocurrency infrastructure is uniquely suited for: \textbf{uniting 
strangers toward a common goal through aligned economic incentives}, with 
no central coordinator required.\\[0.4cm]

Bittensor does not abolish capitalism. It \textbf{purifies it}. It strips 
away the institutional friction, the rent-seeking intermediaries, and the 
misaligned incentive structures that have accumulated over centuries—and 
returns to capitalism's most fundamental logic: \emph{contribute value, 
receive reward}.\\[0.4cm]

Bitcoin demonstrated that money could exist without central banks. Ethereum 
demonstrated that contracts could execute without lawyers. Bittensor 
demonstrates that intelligence can be produced, validated, and compensated 
without corporations. This is not the end of capitalism. It is its
